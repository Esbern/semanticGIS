- **Nominal (N):** Categorical data with no logical order or ranking, used to label variables without quantitative value.
    - _Examples:_ Eye color, country names, gender.
    - _Operations:_ Counting, mode.
- **Ordinal (O):** Categorical data with a clear, inherent ordering or ranking, but the intervals between values are unknown or inconsistent.
    - _Examples:_ Survey responses (satisfied, neutral, dissatisfied), education level, race positions (1st, 2nd).
    - _Operations:_ Median, rank order.
- **Interval (I):** Numerical data where the distance between two values is meaningful and equal. The zero point is arbitrary, meaning it does not represent a complete absence of the value.
    - _Examples:_ Temperature in Celsius or Fahrenheit, time in 12-hour clock.
    - _Operations:_ Addition, subtraction, mean.
- **Ratio (R):** Numerical data that possesses all properties of interval data but includes a true, absolute zero point. The zero point implies the absence of the quantity.
    - _Examples:_ Weight, height, age, income.
    - _Operations:_ Multiplication, division, mean, median. 

Key Characteristics

- **Hierarchy:** Each level includes the characteristics of the ones below it. Ratio is the most complex, while Nominal is the simplest.
- **Qualitative vs. Quantitative:** Nominal and Ordinal are typically categorical (qualitative), while Interval and Ratio are numerical (quantitative).
- **Importance:** Knowing the level of measurement helps determine the statistical tests and data visualization techniques to use.
